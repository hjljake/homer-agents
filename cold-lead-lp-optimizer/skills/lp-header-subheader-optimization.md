# Landing Page Header & Subheader Optimization

The headline and subheadline are the single highest-leverage elements on any landing page. 50–70% of visitors never scroll past the hero. The headline is what earns the scroll. This skill provides frameworks for writing, evaluating, and improving LP headlines and subheadlines.

---

## The Headline's Job

A landing page headline has exactly ONE job: make the right person want to keep reading.

It does this by:
1. **Identifying** the reader (they recognize themselves)
2. **Naming** the outcome they want most
3. **Creating a gap** between where they are and where they could be
4. **Differentiating** from every other option

If the headline does all four in under 3 seconds of reading time, it's working. If it misses even one, conversions suffer.

## Headline Formulas by Intent

### 1. Dream Outcome Formula
**Structure:** `[Achieve specific outcome] + [without common pain / in specific timeframe]`

**Examples:**
- "Get 3x More Qualified Leads Without Increasing Your Ad Spend"
- "Build a Website That Sells — In a Weekend, Not a Quarter"
- "Turn Cold Emails Into Booked Calls in 14 Days"

**When to use:** When the ICP has a clear, specific outcome they're chasing. Best for SaaS, services, and courses. This is the default — use this formula unless there's a strong reason not to.

**Scoring criteria:**
- Is the outcome specific and measurable? ("3x more leads" > "more leads")
- Is the timeline or effort reducer credible? ("14 days" > "instantly")
- Would the ICP use these exact words to describe their goal?

### 2. Pain-Agitation Formula
**Structure:** `[Name the pain] + [agitate by showing consequences] + [hint at resolution]`

**Examples:**
- "Still Losing Deals to Competitors Who Showed Up First?"
- "Your Landing Page Is Costing You $10K/Month in Lost Conversions"
- "Tired of Writing Blog Posts That Nobody Reads?"

**When to use:** When the ICP is problem-aware but not yet solution-aware. Effective for audiences who don't know your product category exists. Use sparingly — too much pain without resolution feels negative.

**Scoring criteria:**
- Does it name a REAL pain the ICP experiences daily?
- Is the agitation specific, not generic?
- Does it create urgency to solve the problem without being manipulative?

### 3. Curiosity Gap Formula
**Structure:** `[Unexpected claim or question] + [specific enough to be credible]`

**Examples:**
- "The 3-Step Framework That Doubled Our Clients' Conversion Rates"
- "Why 73% of Landing Pages Fail (And What the Top 5% Do Differently)"
- "What We Learned From Analyzing 10,000 Landing Pages"

**When to use:** Content-heavy offers (guides, webinars, courses), thought leadership, when the ICP is information-seeking. Less effective for direct purchase pages.

**Scoring criteria:**
- Is the curiosity gap specific enough to be credible?
- Does it promise insider knowledge the ICP can't get elsewhere?
- Would the ICP feel compelled to find out the answer?

### 4. Social Proof Lead Formula
**Structure:** `[Specific proof point] + [implies you can get the same result]`

**Examples:**
- "Join 12,000+ Marketers Who Increased Conversion by 40%"
- "The Platform That Helped Shopify Stores Generate $2.3B in Revenue"
- "Rated #1 by G2 for Landing Page Builders — 4.8/5 From 2,100 Reviews"

**When to use:** When you have strong quantitative proof. Especially effective for cold traffic where trust is low. The proof itself becomes the headline.

**Scoring criteria:**
- Are the numbers specific and impressive? (Precise > rounded)
- Is the proof relevant to the ICP? (Their peers, not random users)
- Does it imply the reader can achieve similar results?

### 5. Question-Based Formula
**Structure:** `[Question that the ICP would answer "yes" to]`

**Examples:**
- "Ready to Stop Guessing What Your Customers Actually Want?"
- "What If You Could Launch Your Online Store This Weekend?"
- "Want to Know Exactly Which Landing Page Elements Are Killing Your Conversions?"

**When to use:** When the ICP has a well-defined problem and is actively looking for solutions. The question should be one they've literally asked themselves.

**Scoring criteria:**
- Would the ICP genuinely answer "yes"?
- Is it specific enough that only the target ICP would relate?
- Does it lead naturally to the product/offer as the answer?

## Subheadline Patterns

The subheadline has a different job than the headline. The headline creates desire. The subheadline removes doubt and builds bridge to the CTA.

### Pattern 1: Doubt Removal
**Structure:** `[Acknowledge the biggest objection] + [neutralize it]`

**Examples:**
- "No coding required. No designers needed. No long contracts."
- "Even if you've never written a landing page before."
- "Without hiring an agency or learning design software."

**When to use:** Always a safe choice. Identify the #1 reason someone would leave after reading the headline and pre-emptively address it.

### Pattern 2: Mechanism Reveal
**Structure:** `[How it works in one sentence] + [why that's different]`

**Examples:**
- "Our AI analyzes your page against 47 conversion criteria and rewrites underperforming elements in real-time."
- "We combine your business data, competitor analysis, and proven frameworks to build pages that convert."
- "A 3-step process: paste your URL, review your score, get specific fixes."

**When to use:** When the product mechanism is a differentiator. Especially effective when the mechanism is simple and the headline promised a complex outcome.

### Pattern 3: Timeline + Specificity
**Structure:** `[Specific outcome] + [in specific timeframe] + [with specific proof]`

**Examples:**
- "Go from idea to published landing page in under 60 minutes — our average user launches in 47."
- "See your first optimization results within 72 hours. Average conversion lift: 23%."
- "Get your personalized growth plan in 5 minutes. Based on data from 10,000+ businesses like yours."

**When to use:** When you have specific data points and the timeline is genuinely fast. Vague timelines ("quickly") are worse than no timeline.

### Pattern 4: Specificity Amplifier
**Structure:** `[Add precise details that make the headline more concrete]`

**Examples:**
- Headline: "Get More Leads From Your Website" → Subheadline: "Our clients see an average 34% increase in form submissions within the first 30 days."
- Headline: "Build Landing Pages That Convert" → Subheadline: "200+ templates. Drag-and-drop editor. Built-in A/B testing. Average 2.4x higher conversion than industry benchmarks."

**When to use:** When the headline is broad/emotional and needs grounding with specifics.

### Pattern 5: Audience Qualifier (Cold Lead Specific)
**Structure:** `For [specific ICP description] who [specific situation]`

**Examples:**
- "For B2B SaaS founders who are getting traffic but not converting it."
- "Built for e-commerce brands doing $50K–$500K/month who want to scale profitably."
- "For marketing teams who are tired of landing pages that look great but don't perform."

**When to use:** When the headline doesn't explicitly name the ICP. The subheadline becomes the qualifier that helps the right person self-select.

## Cold Lead Headline Principles

Cold visitors have zero context. Every word must earn its place.

1. **Zero-Context Clarity**: The headline must make sense to someone who has never heard of you. No product names, no insider terminology, no abbreviations.
2. **ICP Identifier**: Either the headline or subheadline must explicitly identify who this is for. Cold visitors need to self-select in or out.
3. **Differentiation Is Mandatory**: Cold visitors have 5 tabs open. Your headline must say something no competitor could claim.
4. **Trust Injection**: If possible, include a proof element in the headline or subheadline ("Join 10,000+ marketers", "Rated #1 on G2"). Cold visitors need trust at the first line.
5. **Low-Commitment Language**: Don't sell the product in the headline — sell the next action. "See how it works" not "Buy now."

### Cold Lead Headline Checklist
- [ ] Can someone with zero context understand this in 3 seconds?
- [ ] Would the ICP recognize themselves in the first line?
- [ ] Does it name an outcome, not a feature?
- [ ] Could a competitor use this exact headline? (If yes, rewrite)
- [ ] Is there a proof element within the first 2 lines?
- [ ] Does the commitment level match a cold visitor's intent?

---

## Warm Lead Headline Principles

Warm visitors already know you. Don't waste their time re-introducing yourself.

1. **Message Match Is Everything**: The headline MUST continue the conversation started by the email/ad/notification that brought them. If the email said "Your exclusive 30% off," the headline should reference 30% off, not generic welcome copy.
2. **Relationship Acknowledgment**: Signal that you know who they are: "As a [subscriber/customer/member]...", "Because you [action they took]...", "Your exclusive..."
3. **Skip the Preamble**: Don't re-explain who you are or what you do. They know. Get to the offer.
4. **Offer-First Language**: Lead with what they GET, not what you DO. "Your 30% Discount Is Ready" not "We're Offering a Special Deal."
5. **Higher Commitment OK**: Warm visitors can handle more direct CTAs — "Upgrade Now," "Claim Your Offer," "Activate Your Discount." They're further along.

### Warm Lead Headline Checklist
- [ ] Does the headline match the referring message word-for-word where possible?
- [ ] Does it acknowledge the visitor's existing relationship?
- [ ] Is the offer stated clearly in the first 2 lines?
- [ ] Did you avoid re-explaining who you are?
- [ ] Is the language appropriate for someone who already trusts you?

## Before/After Examples by Industry

### SaaS
- **Before:** "The All-In-One Marketing Platform" (generic, feature-focused, no ICP)
- **After:** "Turn Website Visitors Into Paying Customers — Without a Marketing Team" (outcome-focused, ICP qualifier, differentiated)
- **Score impact:** Headline score from 3/12 → 9/12

### E-Commerce
- **Before:** "Shop Our New Collection" (zero specificity, no emotional pull)
- **After:** "Ethically Made Basics That Actually Fit — Starting at $28" (ICP values, specific price, differentiation)
- **Score impact:** Headline score from 2/12 → 8/12

### Professional Services
- **Before:** "Expert Financial Advisory Services" (everyone says this)
- **After:** "Stop Overpaying in Taxes by $12K/Year — We'll Show You Exactly How in a Free 30-Minute Review" (specific outcome, mechanism, risk reversal)
- **Score impact:** Headline score from 2/12 → 10/12

### Local Business
- **Before:** "Welcome to Smith's Plumbing" (about you, not them)
- **After:** "Same-Day Plumbing Repair in [City] — Flat Rate Pricing, No Surprise Fees" (outcome, timeline, risk reversal, local)
- **Score impact:** Headline score from 1/12 → 8/12

## Testing Hierarchy

When A/B testing headlines, test in this order (highest impact first):

1. **Value proposition angle** — Is the outcome the right one? Test completely different angles.
2. **Specificity level** — "More leads" vs "3x more leads" vs "127 more leads per month"
3. **ICP specificity** — Generic audience vs named ICP vs hyper-specific segment
4. **Emotional register** — Aspirational vs pain-point vs curiosity
5. **Voice** — First person ("my") vs second person ("your") vs imperative
6. **Power words** — With urgency words vs without, with numbers vs without
7. **Length** — Short (5-7 words) vs medium (8-12) vs long (13+)

Don't test #7 before you've nailed #1. The angle matters 10x more than word choice.

## Character Count & Readability Guidelines

### Headlines
- **Ideal length:** 6–12 words, 40–70 characters
- **Maximum:** 15 words / 90 characters (beyond this, comprehension drops)
- **Mobile consideration:** Headlines over 60 characters may wrap to 3+ lines on mobile
- **Readability:** Grade 6–8 reading level (Flesch-Kincaid). If a 12-year-old can't understand the gist, simplify.

### Subheadlines
- **Ideal length:** 10–20 words, 60–120 characters
- **Maximum:** 25 words / 150 characters
- **Readability:** Grade 7–9 reading level. Can be slightly more complex than the headline.

### Quick Tests
- **The bar test:** Could you say this headline to someone at a bar and they'd immediately understand what you do and why they should care?
- **The billboard test:** If this were on a billboard at 65mph, would you get it?
- **The "so what?" test:** Read the headline. Ask "so what?" If the answer isn't obvious, rewrite.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Clever over clear.** "Unleash Your Potential" means nothing. "Get 3x More Leads in 30 Days" means everything. Clarity always beats cleverness.
2. **Feature-leading.** "AI-Powered Multi-Channel Attribution Dashboard" — the ICP doesn't care about your technology. They care about the outcome it produces.
3. **Jargon and acronyms.** "Optimize Your CAC:LTV Ratio With Our ABM Platform" — if the headline requires a glossary, it's broken.
4. **Generic superlatives.** "The Best," "World-Class," "Industry-Leading" — every competitor says this. It means nothing.
5. **The "we" problem.** "We help businesses grow" — make it about THEM, not you. "Grow your business by 40% in 90 days."
6. **Missing the ICP.** "For everyone" = for no one. The best headlines explicitly exclude the wrong audience.
7. **Subheadline that repeats the headline.** If the subheadline says the same thing in different words, it's wasted real estate. It should ADD information: doubt removal, mechanism, timeline, or specificity.
8. **Trying to do everything.** A headline that names the ICP AND the outcome AND the mechanism AND the proof AND the timeline is trying too hard. Split across headline + subheadline.

## How to Use This Skill as an Agent

When optimizing headlines/subheadlines:

1. **Start with the current score.** Pull the hero section scores from the scorecard — specifically headline (X/12 or X/8) and subheadline (X/6 or X/4). Identify which criteria scored 0 or 1.

2. **Identify the formula fit.** Based on the ICP, traffic type, and page goal, which headline formula is the best fit? Don't default to Dream Outcome — consider all five.

3. **Generate 3–5 alternatives.** For each, provide:
   - The exact headline text
   - The exact subheadline text
   - Which formula it uses
   - Which scorecard criteria it improves (and by how much)
   - The psychological rationale (why this works for this ICP/traffic type)
   - Projected hero section score improvement

4. **Rank by projected impact.** Put the highest-impact option first. Consider:
   - Which criteria were scoring 0? (Fixing a 0→2 is worth more than a 1→2)
   - Does this option fix the headline AND the subheadline?
   - Is this copy testable in an A/B test?

5. **Provide the testing plan.** For the top 2 recommendations, explain how to A/B test them:
   - Control: current headline
   - Variant: recommended headline
   - What metric to watch (bounce rate, scroll depth, CTA click rate)
   - Minimum sample size
