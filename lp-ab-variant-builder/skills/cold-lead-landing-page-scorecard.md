# Cold Lead Landing Page Scorecard

A grading system for evaluating and building landing pages that convert cold traffic — visitors arriving from paid search, paid social, organic search, display ads, or referral channels who have **no prior relationship** with the brand.

---

## How to Use This Scorecard

Every landing page gets scored across 7 sections. Each section has specific criteria scored 0–2 (0 = missing/broken, 1 = present but weak, 2 = exceptional). Maximum score: 100 points.

**Point allocation is weighted for cold traffic psychology.** Cold visitors need more trust, more proof, and a stronger first impression than warm leads. The hero section and social proof carry disproportionate weight because cold visitors decide in 3–8 seconds whether to stay or bounce.

**Before you score anything, answer these three questions:**

1. Who is the ICP hitting this page? (Be specific: role, revenue range, industry, pain point)
2. What is the single action you want them to take?
3. What is the dream outcome they're chasing?

If you can't answer all three in one sentence each, the page isn't ready to be scored. It's ready to be rewritten.

---

## The Value Equation (Score Every Element Against This)

Every component on the page either increases or decreases perceived value. If an element doesn't serve one of these four levers, it's noise. Cut it.

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)**

The numerator is what makes them want it. The denominator is what makes them hesitate. Great landing pages maximize the top and minimize the bottom.

**For cold leads specifically:** The denominator is naturally HIGH — they don't know you, don't trust you, and assume this will be hard. Your page must aggressively minimize perceived time, effort, and sacrifice while maximizing believability.

## SECTION 1: HERO SECTION (30 points)

*The hero is the entire page for 50–70% of cold visitors. They never scroll. For cold traffic, this section alone determines whether most people stay or bounce. Score it ruthlessly.*

### 1A. Headline (12 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **ICP Identifier**: Does the headline call out the exact person this page is for? Cold visitors need to instantly self-select. Not "business owners" — the specific avatar. "For [exact role/situation] who [specific pain]." | Generic or unclear who it's for | Somewhat targeted but could apply to many audiences | ICP reads it and thinks "this is for me, specifically" — wrong audience self-selects out |
| **Dream Outcome**: Does it name or evoke the result they want most? Not features, not process — the end state they're chasing. Cold visitors have zero context; lead with what they'll get. | Talks about the product/company | Hints at outcomes but leads with features or process | The outcome IS the headline. Clear, specific, desirable. |
| **Clarity in 3 Seconds**: Can someone with zero context read it, understand it, and feel something in under 3 seconds? No jargon, no cleverness that requires thought. Cold traffic is especially unforgiving of confusion. | Confusing, jargon-heavy, or requires context | Clear but takes a moment to process | Instant understanding. A 12-year-old could get the gist. |
| **Emotional Pull**: Does it create urgency, desire, or curiosity? Not through pressure tactics but through the gap between where the reader is and where they want to be. | Flat, informational, no feeling | Some emotional resonance but not compelling | You feel the gap. You want what's on the other side. |
| **Differentiation**: Does it say something only this brand/product could say? Cold visitors are comparison shopping — if any competitor could swap in their logo and the headline still works, it's not differentiated enough. | Completely generic, interchangeable | Has some unique angle but not ownable | Only this brand could say this. It's theirs. |
| **Competitor Contrast**: Is there an implicit or explicit reason why this is different from the 5 other tabs the cold visitor has open? | No differentiation from alternatives | Some unique positioning but not sharpened | Clear "why us, not them" signal — even if subtle |

**Headline Score: ___ / 12**

### 1B. Subheadline (6 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Doubt Removal**: Does it address the biggest objection a cold visitor has? ("without having to...", "even if you've tried...", "in just X days") Cold leads have MORE doubt than warm — this subhead must work harder. | Doesn't address doubts, just restates the headline | Touches on a concern but doesn't resolve it | Directly names and neutralizes the #1 reason a cold visitor would leave |
| **Believability**: Does it make the headline more credible without making it cheesy? Specificity beats superlatives. Numbers beat adjectives. Cold visitors are skeptical by default. | Hype language, unsubstantiated claims | Somewhat credible but relies on vague language | Specific, grounded, you believe it even though you've never heard of this brand |
| **Mechanism or Timeline**: Does it hint at how or how fast? Cold visitors need to understand the path is real and the timeline is reasonable before they'll invest attention. | No indication of how or when | Vague timeline or mechanism | Clear mechanism or timeline that feels achievable |

**Subheadline Score: ___ / 6**

### 1C. CTA Button (6 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Visibility**: Is the CTA impossible to miss? It should be the highest-contrast element on the page. Cold visitors who can't find the CTA in 2 seconds will leave. | Hidden, below fold, or blends in | Visible but not dominant | Unmissable. Your eye goes there immediately. |
| **Copy Specificity**: Does the button say what you GET, not just what you DO? "Get Your Free Analysis" beats "Submit." "Watch the Demo" beats "Learn More." Cold visitors need the value stated on the button itself. | Generic ("Submit", "Click Here", "Learn More") | Somewhat specific but could be sharper | You know exactly what happens when you click, and it sounds valuable |
| **Commitment Level Match**: Is the CTA's commitment level appropriate for a cold visitor? "Buy Now" is too aggressive for someone who just arrived. "See How It Works" or "Get Your Free [X]" matches cold traffic intent. | CTA asks for high commitment from a cold visitor (purchase, long form) | Moderate commitment — reasonable but could be softer | Low-friction entry point that matches where a cold visitor actually is mentally |

**CTA Score: ___ / 6**

### 1D. Trust Anchor in Hero (6 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Presence**: Is there credible proof visible in the hero without scrolling? For cold visitors, this is critical — they need a reason to trust you before they'll read further. Stars, review count, customer logos, testimonial snippet, user count. | No proof in hero section | Proof exists but weak or generic (e.g., "Trusted by thousands") | Specific, credible proof: real numbers, real names, real logos |
| **Relevance**: Does the proof come from people or organizations the ICP would respect? Enterprise logos for enterprise buyers. Founder testimonials for founders. Cold visitors calibrate trust by association. | Proof from irrelevant sources | Somewhat relevant but not ICP-specific | The ICP sees themselves in the proof |
| **Specificity**: Are the numbers real and precise? "4.9 stars from 847 reviews" beats "Trusted by thousands." "Used by 2,300 B2B SaaS companies" beats "Used by many businesses." Cold visitors are pattern-matching for legitimacy. | No specific numbers | Some numbers but vague or rounded | Precise, impressive, verifiable numbers |

**Trust Anchor Score: ___ / 6**

### **TOTAL HERO SECTION SCORE: ___ / 30**

## SECTION 2: BELOW-THE-FOLD SEQUENCE (15 points)

*What they see the moment they scroll. This is where you convert the skeptics — the careful readers who liked the hero but need more before they act. For cold traffic, below-the-fold content must systematically build trust and answer "why should I believe you?"*

### 2A. First Section Below Hero (6 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Momentum**: Does the first section continue the energy of the hero or does it feel like a different page? | Jarring transition, unrelated content | Connected but energy drops | Seamless continuation that deepens the hero's promise |
| **"How It Works" Clarity**: Does this section explain the mechanism? Cold visitors need to understand what happens after they click. 3-step processes, short explainer videos, or visual walkthroughs reduce uncertainty. | No mechanism or process shown | Vague explanation of how it works | Clear 3-step (or similar) explanation that makes the path feel simple and achievable |
| **Visual Engagement**: Is this section visually distinct from the hero while maintaining brand consistency? Screenshots, diagrams, icons, or imagery that breaks up text and keeps cold visitors scrolling. | Wall of text or identical styling to hero | Some visual variety but not compelling | Visually engaging, easy to scan, reinforces the message |

**First Section Score: ___ / 6**

### 2B. Body Sections (6 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Benefit Framing**: Are features presented as benefits? Not "AI-powered analytics" but "Know exactly which decisions are costing you money." Every section answers "so what?" for the reader. | Feature dump with no benefit framing | Mix of features and benefits | Every feature is translated into an outcome the ICP cares about |
| **Objection Handling**: Does the page systematically address the top 3–5 objections a COLD visitor would have? ("Is this legit?" "Will it work for me?" "What if it doesn't work?" "Is it worth the effort?") | Objections ignored | Some objections addressed but not all major ones | Top cold-visitor objections are anticipated and neutralized before they become blockers |
| **Scannability**: Can someone who won't read every word still get the full message from headlines, subheads, and bolded text alone? Cold visitors scan aggressively — they won't read paragraphs. | Requires full read to understand the offer | Partially scannable | Headlines alone tell the complete story |

**Body Sections Score: ___ / 6**

### 2C. Repeated CTAs (3 points)

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Frequency**: Is the CTA repeated at natural decision points? After proof, after benefits, after objection handling. Not just top and bottom. | CTA only in hero and/or footer | 2 CTA placements | CTA appears at every natural decision point (3+ placements) |
| **Consistency**: Is it the same action every time? One page, one goal. The CTA should never compete with itself. | Multiple competing CTAs or actions | Mostly consistent with minor distractions | One action, reinforced every time, zero competing options |

Note: Score out of 4 possible, weighted to 3. Use best judgment.

**Repeated CTAs Score: ___ / 3**

## SECTION 3: SOCIAL PROOF DEPTH (20 points)

*For cold traffic, proof is EVERYTHING. This section carries more weight than for warm leads because cold visitors have zero prior trust. The more specific, varied, and relevant your proof, the higher the perceived likelihood of achievement in the value equation.*

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Testimonial Quality**: Are testimonials specific about results, not just "great product"? Do they mention before/after, timelines, specific outcomes? Cold visitors need to see themselves in these stories. | No testimonials or generic praise | Testimonials exist but lack specificity | Specific results: "went from X to Y in Z time" with named, photographed people |
| **Testimonial Diversity**: Do testimonials represent different ICPs, use cases, or stages? Multiple testimonials covering different objections build broader trust than one amazing quote. | None or only one | A few but similar angles | Multiple testimonials addressing different concerns, industries, or use cases |
| **Visual Proof**: Are there screenshots, dashboards, before/after images, or video that show the product/result in action? Seeing is believing — especially for cold visitors who can't verify claims. | No visual proof | Some visuals but generic or stock | Real product screenshots, real results, real demonstrations |
| **Authority Proof**: Logos, press mentions, certifications, partnerships, investor names, or other signals that say "serious people trust this." Cold visitors use authority signals as mental shortcuts. | No authority signals | Some logos or mentions but not prominently placed | Credible authority signals that the ICP would recognize and respect |
| **Quantitative Proof**: Specific numbers: "1,300% MRR growth", "10x valuation in 5 months", "500+ companies", "4.9 stars from 200 reviews." Numbers are more believable than adjectives — especially to skeptical cold visitors. | No numbers | Some numbers but vague ("thousands of customers") | Specific, impressive, verifiable numbers |
| **Case Study or Deep Proof**: At least one detailed example of a customer journey — who they were, what happened, where they are now. Cold visitors need narrative proof, not just stats. | No case study or deep example | Brief mention of results without depth | Compelling narrative: transformation story with specifics the ICP can relate to |
| **Third-Party Validation**: Reviews on independent platforms (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, Google Reviews), press coverage, or industry awards. Cold visitors trust third-party sources more than self-reported proof. | No third-party validation | Mentioned but not linked or shown | Prominent third-party scores/reviews with links to verify |
| **Proof Variety**: Does the page use at least 3 DIFFERENT types of proof (testimonials, logos, numbers, case studies, reviews, press, video)? Variety creates compound credibility for cold visitors. | Only 1 type of proof | 2 types of proof | 3+ distinct types of proof, strategically distributed |
| **Proof Placement**: Is proof distributed throughout the page (hero, after benefits, before CTA) or dumped in one "testimonials" section? | All proof in one isolated section | Some distribution but not strategic | Proof appears exactly where doubt would arise |
| **Recency**: Does the proof feel current? Dated testimonials or old logos suggest the product's best days are behind it. Cold visitors are especially sensitive to staleness. | Visibly outdated proof | No dates shown (neutral) | Clearly recent, current, or continuously updated |

**TOTAL SOCIAL PROOF SCORE: ___ / 20**

## SECTION 4: OFFER CLARITY (10 points)

*If a cold visitor can't explain what they're getting and what it costs within 10 seconds of looking at the page, the offer is unclear. Cold visitors have less patience for ambiguity than warm leads.*

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **What You Get**: Is it crystal clear what the visitor receives? Not vague promises but specific deliverables, access, or outcomes. Cold visitors need this spelled out explicitly — they can't fill in gaps from prior experience with the brand. | Unclear what you actually get | Somewhat clear but requires interpretation | Unmistakable. You know exactly what you're getting. |
| **Pricing Transparency** (if applicable): If there's a price, is it presented confidently with clear value framing? If free, is the value of what's free made obvious? Cold visitors are price-sensitive because they haven't built trust yet. | Price hidden, confusing, or apologetic | Price shown but without value context | Price presented with confidence, anchored against higher value |
| **Risk Reversal**: Is there a guarantee, free trial, money-back promise, or other risk reducer? For cold visitors, this is CRITICAL — they're taking a chance on an unknown brand. The risk reversal must be prominent and generous. | No risk reversal of any kind | Weak or buried guarantee | Prominent, generous risk reversal that removes the last barrier — visible near every CTA |
| **Urgency or Scarcity** (if used): If there's urgency, is it real? Fake countdown timers DESTROY trust with cold visitors who are already skeptical. Real scarcity (limited spots, cohort-based, seasonal) increases action. | Fake urgency or manipulative scarcity | No urgency used (neutral, score 1) | Genuine, believable urgency that motivates action without eroding trust |
| **Single Clear Path**: Is there one thing to do on this page? Cold visitors are easily distracted by competing menus, links, footer navigation, and "about us" distractions. Remove everything that isn't the CTA. | Multiple competing paths and distractions | Mostly focused but some unnecessary exits | One page, one action, zero distractions. Navigation removed or minimized. |

**TOTAL OFFER CLARITY SCORE: ___ / 10**

## SECTION 5: MOBILE EXPERIENCE (10 points)

*The majority of cold traffic — especially from paid social and display — is mobile. If the page doesn't work on a phone, your ad spend is wasted.*

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Load Speed**: Does the page load in under 3 seconds on mobile? Every additional second costs ~7% of conversions. Cold visitors from paid channels have essentially zero patience. | Over 5 seconds | 3–5 seconds | Under 3 seconds |
| **Hero on Mobile**: Is the full hero (headline, subhead, CTA) visible on a phone screen without scrolling? Content should re-stack, not shrink. The CTA must be visible on the first mobile screen. | Hero requires scrolling to see CTA | Most of hero visible but CTA is cut off | Complete hero visible on first mobile screen |
| **Tap Targets**: Are buttons large enough to tap with a thumb? Minimum 48px height (Google), ideally 56px+. Enough spacing between interactive elements to prevent mis-taps. | Small buttons, crowded tap targets | Mostly tappable but some tight spacing | All buttons are thumb-friendly with generous spacing |
| **Readability**: Is text large enough to read without zooming? 16px minimum body text. Line length appropriate for mobile (not spanning full width). | Text too small, lines too long | Readable but not optimized | Perfect readability, comfortable line length, clear hierarchy |
| **Form Friction**: If there's a form, is it minimal? Every field reduces completion. For cold visitors: name + email maximum. Phone number costs ~5% completion per field. Each additional field beyond 2 drops conversion ~10%. | Long form with unnecessary fields (5+) | Reasonable but could be shorter (3–4 fields) | Minimal fields: only what's absolutely necessary (1–2 fields) |

**TOTAL MOBILE SCORE: ___ / 10**

## SECTION 6: BRAND IDENTITY & DESIGN (10 points)

*Cold visitors form brand impressions in milliseconds. The design must signal professionalism, trustworthiness, and relevance — before a single word is read.*

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **Professional Polish**: Does the page look like it was built by a real company? Cold visitors use design quality as a proxy for product quality. Template-looking pages with stock imagery signal "fly-by-night." | Looks like a generic template or unfinished | Decent but some rough edges | Polished, intentional, clearly invested in |
| **Emotional Design**: Does the visual design create the feeling the brand wants to evoke? Premium, playful, bold, trustworthy — whatever the brand's emotional target. Cold visitors feel the brand before they read it. | No emotional resonance, purely functional | Some emotional design but not fully realized | The design makes you feel what the brand wants you to feel |
| **Photography/Imagery**: Are images real, human, and relevant? Generic stock photos are invisible to cold visitors. Real team photos, product shots, or custom illustrations create authenticity. | Generic stock photos or no imagery | Some real imagery mixed with stock | All imagery feels authentic, real, and relevant to the ICP |
| **White Space & Breathing Room**: Does the page feel spacious and intentional or cramped and desperate? Cramped pages signal desperation to cold visitors. | Cluttered, cramped, overwhelming | Adequate spacing but could breathe more | Spacious, intentional, every element has room |
| **Typography**: Are headlines and body text clearly distinct? Is the type hierarchy guiding the eye? Readable and on-brand? Cold visitors rely on typography hierarchy to scan quickly. | Poor type choices, no hierarchy | Decent typography but not distinctive | Typography reinforces brand personality and guides the eye perfectly |

**TOTAL BRAND/DESIGN SCORE: ___ / 10**

## SECTION 7: TECHNICAL & TRUST FUNDAMENTALS (5 points)

*These are table stakes. If any of these are broken, nothing else matters — and cold visitors are less forgiving of technical issues because they have no prior trust to draw on.*

| Criteria | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| **SSL/Security**: Does the page have HTTPS and relevant trust badges (especially near forms and payment)? Cold visitors who see "Not Secure" in the browser bar will leave immediately. | No HTTPS or trust indicators | HTTPS but no trust badges near forms | HTTPS + trust badges + security indicators where needed |
| **No Broken Elements**: Do all images load? Do all buttons work? Do all forms submit? Does the page render correctly? A single broken element tells a cold visitor the company doesn't care about details. | Broken elements present | Minor rendering issues | Everything works perfectly |
| **Privacy/Legal**: Is there a privacy policy link near form fields? GDPR/CCPA compliance if applicable? Cold visitors are more cautious about giving data to unknown brands. | No privacy policy or legal links | Privacy policy exists but not near forms | Privacy link near forms, clear data usage language, trust-building copy ("We'll never spam you") |

Note: 6 points possible, weighted to 5. Deduct if anything is broken.

### Grading Scale

**90–100: World-Class.** This page is doing everything right for cold traffic. Small optimizations through A/B testing are the only upside left. Ship it and let data refine it.

**75–89: Strong.** The fundamentals are solid. There are 1–3 areas holding it back from elite cold-traffic conversion. Fix those and you're in the 90s.

**60–74: Decent Foundation, Significant Gaps.** The page works but it's leaving cold-traffic conversions on the table. Usually the hero is weak, proof is thin, or the risk reversal is missing. Pick the lowest-scoring section and fix that first.

**40–59: Needs Major Work.** Multiple sections are underperforming. Cold visitors are bouncing because trust hasn't been established. Go back to basics: who is this for, what do they want, why should they believe you (with PROOF), and what do you want them to do? Rebuild from the Value Equation up.

**Below 40: Start Over.** The page isn't converting cold traffic because it hasn't earned the right to ask for anything. Cold visitors have zero patience for unclear, untrustworthy, or unfocused pages. Rewrite with the ICP, the single action, and TRUST-BUILDING as the foundation.

## COLD LEAD ANTI-PATTERNS

These mistakes are especially lethal for cold traffic:

1. **Assuming they know you.** Cold visitors have never heard of your brand. Don't skip the "what we do" — state it clearly in the hero.
2. **Leading with features.** Cold visitors don't care about your technology stack. They care about their problem and your outcome.
3. **No social proof above the fold.** If a cold visitor doesn't see proof in the hero, they assume there is none.
4. **Asking for too much too soon.** A cold visitor won't fill out a 7-field form or enter their credit card. Reduce friction: name + email, or even just email.
5. **Using insider jargon.** Your product terminology means nothing to someone who's never used it. Use the language your ICP uses to describe their problem.
6. **No risk reversal anywhere.** Cold visitors need to know they can't lose by trying. No guarantee = too much risk for an unknown brand.
7. **Competing with yourself.** Multiple CTAs, navigation links, footer menus — every exit point is a leak in your cold-traffic funnel.
8. **Fake urgency.** Countdown timers and "only 3 left!" on a digital product. Cold visitors are skeptical; fake urgency confirms their suspicion that you're not trustworthy.
9. **The "About Us" trap.** Dedicating page real estate to your founding story before establishing value. Cold visitors don't care about you yet — they care about themselves.
10. **Sending paid traffic to a homepage.** Homepages serve many audiences. Cold traffic from a specific ad needs a dedicated landing page that continues the ad's promise.

## HOW TO USE THIS AS AN AGENT SKILL

When scoring a landing page as an AI agent:

1. **Gather context first.** Before scoring, determine: the ICP (who is this for?), the offer (what do they get?), the goal (what action should they take?), the traffic source (where are cold visitors coming from?), and the brand identity (what should they feel?).

2. **Check for message match.** If the visitor is coming from a specific ad, does the landing page headline match the ad's promise? Message mismatch is the #1 cold-traffic conversion killer.

3. **Score the hero section first and separately.** If the hero scores below 20/30, flag it as the priority fix before touching anything else. The hero is 80% of the page's impact for cold traffic.

4. **Be specific in your scoring notes.** Don't just say "headline is weak." Say "the headline talks about the product's AI capabilities instead of naming the outcome the ICP wants. Rewrite to lead with the dream outcome: [specific suggestion]."

5. **Provide a rewrite for any element scoring 0 or 1.** The scorecard is diagnostic, but the value is in the prescription. Every low score should come with a suggested fix.

6. **Score mobile independently.** Pull up the page on mobile (or evaluate the mobile layout) and score Section 5 separately. A page that's a 90 on desktop and a 50 on mobile is a 50 — especially for cold traffic from paid social which is 70–90% mobile.

7. **Prioritize fixes by section weight.** Hero (30 pts) > Social Proof (20 pts) > Below-fold (15 pts) > Offer + Mobile + Brand (10 each) > Technical (5 pts). Fix the highest-weighted low scores first.

8. **Evaluate the ad-to-page journey.** Don't score the page in isolation. Consider: does the page deliver on whatever promise brought the cold visitor here? If the ad says "Free consultation" and the page says "Contact us for pricing," there's a disconnect that no amount of page optimization can fix.

## QUICK-REFERENCE: THE 11/10 COLD-TRAFFIC HERO

For when you need to nail the hero and nothing else matters yet:

**Headline**: Names the ICP's dream outcome in their language. Specific enough that the wrong person would self-select out. Clear in under 3 seconds. Only this brand could say it. A cold visitor instantly knows what this is and who it's for.

**Subheadline**: Removes the #1 doubt a stranger would have. Adds a mechanism or timeline. Makes the headline more believable without cheesiness. Specificity over superlatives.

**CTA**: Unmissable. Highest contrast element on the page. Says what you GET, not what you DO. Commitment level matches where a cold visitor actually is (not "Buy Now" — more like "See How It Works" or "Get Your Free [X]").

**Trust Anchor**: Real proof visible without scrolling. Not "trusted by thousands" but "4.9 stars from 847 reviews" or "used by 2,300 founders." Logo bar of recognizable companies. A specific testimonial snippet with a real name and photo.

**Visual/Brand**: The page looks like it was built by a real, successful company. The design signals the brand's personality. The visual hierarchy is headline > CTA > trust anchor > everything else. A cold visitor's first impression is "this is legit."

**The test**: Show the hero to someone who has never heard of this brand for 5 seconds. Take it away. Ask them: what is this, who is it for, why should I trust them, and what should I do? If they can answer all four, you've got an 11.
